Standing on Mt Elbert
Zen of Running
Movement without effort, awareness without thinking. This is the zen of running. I've found that achieving this state requires simplicity. Simplicity means saying no to habits and gadgets common to many runners.
No music players or headphones. No stopwatches or split times. No GPS watches or pace tracking. No heart-rate monitors. No mobile phones or touchscreen devices, velcro-strapped to one arm. And certainly no chest-mounted GoPro cameras.
Gadgets, although in some cases helpful, become distractions. I've found that it's impossible to achieve a zen state of running with any gadgets present. But there's a bit more to simplicity than unplugging: it's also necessary to let go of training goals and expectations--and this goes hand-in-hand with leaving gadgets behind. After all, how could I measure split times or pace without a chonometer?
The pure essense of running is the act itself. Putting one foot in front of the other. Moving through space. And I think it matters what that space is: I've never achieved a zen state of running on a treadmill, or doing circles around a track. Outside, especially on a sunny day, with mountains in view, away from a city and its noise, it's pretty easy to achieve a zen state of running. In fact, with cooperative weather and a well-chosen route, it's all but guaranteed to happen.
One Number
Running can generate a lot of numbers, especially when someone is training for (or participating in) a race. Pace, distance, mile splits, elevation gained and lost, to name only a few. However, in my run log, I only record one number: distance.
Even though I rarely wear a watch when running, I am always aware of the distance of of my runs. I know the distance because I usually run known routes. When I'm running an unknown route, I use a GPS watch to measure distance, after which the route becomes known to me, and thereafter I can run the route without GPS.
I find that running known routes without a watch allows me to focus on the pure essense of running, while still allowing me log distance for later analysis.
Analysis & Charts
Now that I have almost 3 years of run log entries, my data can answer some interesting questions. Here are some examples:
How many miles per month have I run, in 2013? 2012? 2011? All time? What is the average run distance for each day of the week? On average, how many times do I run in a given month? Summer vs. winter? What fraction of my runs are over ten miles in distance? What fraction of my runs are on roads? Trails? What footwear do I most commonly run in?
A lot of possibilities arise from date, distance, surface and footwear information. As I develop this site more, I plan to feature charts answering the above questions and more.
